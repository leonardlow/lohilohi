Roadmap

The master plan — from content creator to business owner. Phased milestones, honest checkpoints, and what success actually looks like.

Where we are now Alohi is evaluating a major life decision — leaving a $60K/year job by July 17th. This roadmap exists to make sure that decision is backed by a real business, not just momentum.

Phase 1 — Foundation (Now → July 17)

Before anything else, the basics have to be in place. This phase is about getting the business structure right so there's something real to jump into.

  • Review and understand the agency contract in full
  • Determine what the agency actually delivers vs. what it costs
  • Form an LLC or confirm existing legal structure
  • Open a dedicated business bank account
  • Set up a business email address
  • Create a real rate card for brand deals
  • Establish a baseline of monthly income and actual expenses
  • Decide: quit July 17th, or push the date with a clearer target?

Phase 2 — Systems (Months 1–3 Post-Job)

Once she's full-time, the focus shifts to building repeatable revenue systems — not just posting and hoping for deposits.

  • Brand deal pipeline: 25–50 outbound pitches per week
  • Merch: launch first product or limited drop
  • Streaming: optimize the 65-hour commitment for actual revenue
  • Accounting: track every expense as a potential write-off
  • Quarterly tax payments begin (self-employment = ~25–30%)
  • Measure: is monthly revenue on pace to match or exceed $60K/year?

Phase 3 — Growth (Months 4–12)

With systems running, the goal becomes compounding — more recurring revenue, fewer one-off deals, and real business independence.

  • Convert brand deals into retainers and ambassador programs
  • Expand merch into a consistent product line
  • Evaluate convention appearances as a revenue stream
  • Build toward Patreon or membership community
  • Review agency relationship: is it still worth the commission?
  • Year-end: file taxes with full write-offs, assess net profitability

Key Decision Points

  • July 17, 2026 — Potential last day at full-time job. Go/no-go decision.
  • Month 3 — Is revenue consistent enough to sustain? Adjust or press on.
  • Month 6 — Agency contract review: renegotiate, renew, or exit?
  • Year 1 — Full tax year as a business. Real profitability assessment.

Business Foundation

The legal and structural backbone of the business. This is what separates a hobby from a company — and what protects Alohi personally if something goes wrong.

Why this matters first Right now, Alohi is likely operating as a sole proprietor with no legal separation between her personal finances and her business. That means personal liability, no clear business credit, and tax headaches. This section fixes that.

Legal Structure

What type of business entity is this? Most creators start as sole proprietors by default — but an LLC provides liability protection and looks more credible to brands.

  • Has an LLC been formed? In what state?
  • Articles of Organization / LLC formation documents
  • Registered agent information
  • Operating agreement (even for a single-member LLC)
  • EIN (Employer Identification Number) — needed for bank account and taxes

Business Banking

All business income and expenses must flow through a separate business account. Mixing personal and business money is one of the most common mistakes young creators make — it makes taxes a nightmare and undermines the LLC protection.

  • Business checking account (separate from personal)
  • Business credit or debit card for expenses
  • PayPal or payment processor linked to business account
  • Process for moving income from TikTok/Instagram to business account

Business Identity

The professional face of the business when dealing with brands and agencies.

  • Business email address (not personal Gmail)
  • Media kit / one-pager for brand outreach
  • Standard invoice template
  • W-9 form on file (brands require this before payment)

Insurance

Often overlooked by creators — but relevant once income is real.

  • Health insurance (no longer covered by employer after July 17th)
  • Business liability insurance
  • Equipment / gear insurance for cosplay production assets

Contract

The agency agreement — what Alohi has signed, what she owes them, what they owe her, and what it takes to get out if needed.

Agency call — date TBD The goal of that call is to fully understand the contract before making any major decisions — especially before July 17th. Drop the contract document and call notes here afterward.

Questions to Get Answered on the Call

These are the non-negotiables. Every answer should be documented in the Calls tab.

  • What is the exact commission rate? On which types of income?
  • Does commission apply to creator-sourced deals or only agency-sourced?
  • Does commission apply to TikTok/Instagram direct monetization?
  • Does commission apply to merch sales?
  • What is the contract term length? When does it expire?
  • What is the exit clause? How many days notice? Any penalties?
  • Who owns the content IP — Alohi or the agency?
  • Is there an exclusivity clause? What does it cover?
  • What exactly is the 65-hour streaming requirement? Is it enforceable?
  • What happens if she doesn't hit 65 hours in a given month?
  • What is the agency actively doing to generate deal flow right now?

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Commission on ALL revenue regardless of who sourced the deal
  • Long exclusivity clauses with no carve-outs
  • Agency claims ownership of any content or brand identity
  • No clear exit clause or exit requires agency approval
  • Vague performance expectations with no accountability on their side
  • Automatic renewal language buried in the agreement

Acceptable Terms (Reference)

  • Commission: 20% maximum on agency-sourced deals only
  • 0–10% on creator-sourced deals
  • Exit clause: 30–60 days notice, no financial penalty
  • IP: Alohi retains full ownership of all content and brand identity
  • No exclusivity on merch, direct fan income, or personal ventures

Documents

  • Current agency agreement (to be added after call)
  • Any amendments or addendums
  • The 65-hour streaming clause in writing
  • Commission schedule breakdown

Finances

The real financial picture — not just what comes in, but what actually stays after expenses, taxes, and costs that may currently be invisible.

The hard truth A creator can look profitable while someone else is quietly absorbing the costs. Costumes, props, equipment, software, convention travel — if those aren't tracked as business expenses, the "profit" number is fiction. This section makes the real number visible.

Income Streams

Every source of revenue, tracked separately so we know what's actually working.

  • TikTok Creator Fund / TikTok LIVE gifts and subscriptions
  • Instagram monetization
  • Brand deal payments (agency-sourced)
  • Brand deal payments (self-sourced)
  • Merch sales (net after platform fees)
  • Convention appearances / paid events
  • Patreon or membership income (future)

Business Expenses

These are real costs of doing business — they reduce taxable income and must be tracked. If someone else is paying them, that is a loan or a gift, not free money.

  • Costumes, props, and cosplay materials
  • Camera, lighting, and production equipment
  • Editing software and subscriptions
  • Convention registration and travel
  • Home office / studio space allocation
  • Hair, makeup, styling for content
  • Shipping and packaging (for merch)
  • Business email, website, and tools
  • Agency commission (deducted from gross)

The Salary Replacement Target

Alohi's current employment income is approximately $60,000/year ($5,000/month gross). To replace that as a self-employed creator, she needs to earn significantly more because:

  • Self-employment tax is ~15.3% on top of income tax
  • No employer-sponsored health insurance
  • No 401(k) match or paid time off
  • Inconsistent monthly income — need a buffer
  • Target gross revenue: $80,000–$90,000/year to net equivalent take-home

Externally Fronted Costs

Costs currently being covered by others must be logged here. This is not about guilt — it's about building an accurate picture of what the business actually costs to run.

  • Equipment purchased on behalf of Alohi
  • Costume and production costs covered externally
  • Any travel, convention, or event costs fronted
  • Software or subscription costs paid by others

Accounting

The paper trail — every dollar in and out, documented. This is what gets handed to a CPA at tax time and what protects Alohi in an audit.

Self-employment tax is real As a self-employed creator, Alohi pays both the employee AND employer share of Social Security and Medicare — roughly 15.3% on top of regular income tax. Proper accounting with write-offs is how that number gets smaller.

Invoices

Every brand deal should have an invoice sent before or upon payment. Invoices protect Alohi legally and create a clear paper trail.

  • Invoice template (reusable, with her business name and EIN)
  • Sent invoices by month and brand
  • Payment status tracking (sent, pending, paid)
  • Late payment follow-up log

Expense Receipts

Every business expense needs a receipt. These are the write-offs that reduce taxable income — possibly saving thousands per year. The IRS expects documentation.

  • Costume and prop purchases
  • Camera, lighting, microphone, and production gear
  • Editing software (Adobe, DaVinci, etc.)
  • Convention registration fees
  • Travel: flights, hotels, rideshare (for business purposes)
  • Hair, makeup, and styling for content production
  • Home office: portion of rent/utilities if applicable
  • Business subscriptions and software tools
  • Merch production and shipping costs

Quarterly Tax Estimates

Self-employed creators must pay estimated taxes 4x per year. Missing these results in penalties. Dates to know:

  • Q1: April 15
  • Q2: June 16
  • Q3: September 15
  • Q4: January 15 (following year)
  • Set aside ~25–30% of every payment received

Annual Records

  • 1099-NEC forms (from brands that paid $600+ in a year)
  • TikTok and platform earnings statements
  • Year-end profit and loss summary
  • Prior year tax returns (for reference)
  • CPA contact and annual filing calendar

Brand Deals

Sponsorship pipeline — from first contact to closed deal. The goal is to treat this like sales, not luck. Consistent outreach, confident pricing, and repeat business.

The biggest opportunity right now Alohi has 1M+ TikTok followers and is under-monetized. Brand deals are the fastest path to real income. The key is being proactive — most creators wait for inbound. The ones who win send 25–50 pitches a week.

Rate Card

Starting rates — state these confidently and never apologize for them. Never immediately discount; reduce scope instead.

  • Single TikTok post: $5,000
  • TikTok + Instagram bundle: $6,500–$8,000
  • Usage rights (brand can repurpose content): +30–100%
  • Exclusivity (can't work with competitors): +20–50%
  • TikTok LIVE integration: negotiate separately

Target Brand Categories

Brands most aligned with Alohi's audience and content niche.

  • Anime merchandise and figure manufacturers
  • Gaming companies and streaming platforms
  • Cosplay and costume suppliers
  • Apparel and streetwear brands
  • Convention organizers (Anime Expo, Fan Expo, etc.)
  • Collectibles and limited edition releases
  • Beauty and makeup brands
  • Tech accessories (LED, ring lights, stream gear)

Pipeline Stages

  • Prospecting — brands identified, not yet contacted
  • Outreach sent — pitch email or DM sent, awaiting response
  • In conversation — brand responded, discussing scope/budget
  • Negotiating — offer on the table, counter in progress
  • Closed / Won — deal signed, invoice sent
  • Delivered — content posted, payment received
  • Follow-up — post-campaign check-in for repeat business

Inbound Inquiry Response

Every brand inquiry gets this response first — it filters low-budget leads and establishes professionalism before any pricing is shared:

  • Thanks for reaching out — what are the campaign goals?
  • What deliverables are you looking for?
  • What's the timeline?
  • What's the budget range?
  • Are usage rights or exclusivity required?

Merch

Alohi's fully owned product line — independent of the agency, no commission owed to anyone. This is the revenue stream she builds and keeps entirely.

Why merch is different from brand deals Brand deals are income she earns by promoting someone else's product. Merch is a business she owns — she sets the prices, controls the brand, and keeps the margins. No agency cut. No approval needed.

Product Concepts

Starting ideas based on her existing audience and cosplay identity. Limited drops tied to viral moments tend to perform best.

  • Cosplay-themed apparel (hoodies, tees, crop tops)
  • Prints and art prints from popular cosplay shoots
  • Accessories (pins, keychains, phone cases) tied to specific fandoms
  • Limited edition drops around convention appearances
  • Signed memorabilia for superfans
  • Digital products: cosplay guides, preset packs, tutorials

Fulfillment Options

Print-on-demand means no upfront inventory cost — ideal for starting out. As volume grows, direct fulfillment improves margins.

  • Printify — print-on-demand, integrates with Shopify, low risk
  • Printful — similar to Printify, strong quality control
  • Shopify — storefront platform, owns the customer relationship
  • Spring (formerly Teespring) — creator-native, lower setup friction
  • Direct fulfillment — higher margin, requires inventory management

Margin Awareness

Merch is only worth pursuing if the numbers make sense. Every product needs a margin analysis before launch.

  • Product cost (printing + materials)
  • Platform fee (Shopify, Printify, etc.)
  • Shipping cost to customer
  • Payment processing fee (~3%)
  • Retail price minus all of the above = actual margin
  • Target: 40–60% margin minimum

Launch Checklist

  • Define first product or limited drop concept
  • Choose fulfillment platform
  • Set up storefront (Shopify or equivalent)
  • Run margin analysis before pricing
  • Create launch content tied to existing audience
  • Link store in TikTok and Instagram bio
  • Set up order tracking and customer email
