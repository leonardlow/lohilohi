Meeting with Warens

This is an introduction, not a negotiation. The contracts are in place. The goal is to establish a relationship with Warens as Alohi's family — to let him know she has people behind her who are supportive, engaged, and trusting in him to do right by her.

The message we want to leave him with: We are supportive parents who love our daughter and want the best for her career. We trust you to be honorable and treat her fairly. We're here to learn, not to challenge anything.

What this meeting is not: A contract review. A negotiation. An interrogation. Keep it warm, keep it brief, let him talk.

Opening — Set the Tone

Let the first few minutes be about relationship, not business. Introduce yourselves, acknowledge what he's doing for Alohi, and make clear why you're there.

"Warens, thank you for taking the time to meet with us. We're Alohi's parents — we've been watching her work really hard at this and we just want to be more informed and supportive. We're not here to get in the way of anything, we just want to understand the world she's in a little better."

This is an opening statement, not a question. Say it early and mean it. It sets the entire tone of the meeting. Let there be a pause after — he'll fill it, and what he says next tells you a lot about how he views the relationship.

His Vision for Alohi

Let him lead. A question this open gives him room to show whether he has a real plan or just a contract.

"Where do you see Alohi going over the next year? What's your vision for her?"

The most important question of the meeting. Listen for specifics — brands, platforms, revenue goals, milestones. Generalities mean he hasn't thought about her individually. A real answer means he has a plan.

Understanding How It Works

Frame this as parents trying to understand — not as people reviewing the contract. Let him explain the process in his own words.

"Can you walk us through how the partnership works day to day? How does Alohi work with you, and what does that communication look like?"

This tells you how active they actually are and what Alohi's experience of the relationship is in practice. Compare his answer to what she told you.

"We want to help Alohi understand her finances better as she grows. Can you walk us through how the commission structure works — just so we can help her plan and budget properly?"

Coming from a parenting angle — helping her manage money — not questioning whether the split is fair. Let him explain the 15%/10%/1.5% breakdown himself. Note whether he discloses that both percentages go to him.

"We know she earns income directly from TikTok LIVE and her other platforms — is that income separate from the agency relationship, or does it factor in?"

Phrased as clarification, not a challenge. Confirms whether he claims commission on direct platform income. His answer should match what Alohi told you.

What's Coming for Alohi

Show genuine interest in her opportunities — not skepticism about past activity.

"Is there anything in the works right now that we should know about — any brand conversations or opportunities coming up for her?"

Warm and forward-looking. What he volunteers here — without being pushed — is the clearest signal of whether the pipeline is real. Don't follow up with pressure if the answer is thin. Just note it.

"Alohi has been talking about launching some of her own merchandise — things she designs and sells herself. Is that something that works alongside what you're doing, or is there anything we need to be aware of?"

Positioned as asking for guidance, not testing the exclusivity clause. His answer confirms whether merch is in or out of his scope — which is what you need to know before she launches anything.

Closing — Building the Relationship

End the meeting by making him a partner, not a subject. Leave him feeling respected and trusted.

"As her family getting more involved — is there anything you need from us, or anything we can do to help support what you're building with her?"

This is the most disarming question in the room. It puts him in the position of being a partner rather than someone being evaluated. His answer will either show genuine collaboration or reveal that he's not used to thinking about the creator's family as part of the equation.

"Just so we understand the road ahead — when it comes time to make decisions about continuing the partnership, what does that process look like and what's the timeline?"

Administrative and forward-looking. Gets the non-renewal timeline confirmed without framing it as an exit conversation. Save this for the very end — after the relationship is warm.
